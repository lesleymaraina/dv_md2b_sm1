Categorical Data Review: Definition

  • Categorical data is often coded data that could consist of constructed labels, designed for communication and record-keeping within specific institutional systems

  • Encodings often have a specific purpose within a system

  • Example:

    • ICD-10 codes: billing, mortality statistics
    • CTCAE grades: adverse event grading

Categorical Data Review: Definition

  • Often requires translation for analysis and can be summarized using counts or ratios

  • Example

    • Hundreds of ICD-10 codes:
      • E11.9 - type 2 diabetes mellitus without complications

Categorical Variables are produced by different systems

  • Genomic science
  • Diagnostics
  • Treatments/exposures
  • Billing

Systems often use internal logic to generate categorical labels

  • What counts as a valid value
  • How and whether values are ordered
  • Whether multiple values of a unit can coexist
  • Defining “unknown” within a category

Common data scources in biomedical and clinical science

Genomic Data

Definition and Scope:

  • Information about an individual’s DNA sequence, including variations in genes, regulatory elements, and sometimes RNA or epigenetic modifications
  • Source: typically generated through high-throughput sequencing technologies like whole-genome sequencing (WGS), whole-exome sequencing (WES), or RNA sequencing

Types of Genomic Data:

  • Variant Data: Single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), insertions/deletions, structural variants

  • Expression Data: RNA sequencing provides information about gene expression levels

  • Epigenomic Data: Methylation patterns, chromatin accessibility, etc., which regulate gene expression without altering DNA sequence

Common data scources in biomedical and clinical science

Genomic Data

Uses in Research:

  • Understanding disease mechanisms at the molecular level.

  • Identifying genetic risk factors for complex diseases.

  • Pharmacogenomics: predicting patient response to medications based on genotype

  • Integrative analyses, combining EHR clinical phenotypes with genomic profiles to discover genotype-phenotype relationships

Challenges:

  • High dimensionality: millions of variants per individual

  • Need for robust computational infrastructure and bioinformatics expertise

Genomic data source

dbGaP - database of Genotypes and Phenotypes (dbGaP) is an NIH-managed platform that curates and distributes genomic and phenotypic data from large-scale human studies

  • Reseource that allows researchers to integrate and reanalyze datasets while maintaining rigorous standards for participant consent and data security

UK Biobank - large-scale biomedical research resource that has collected detailed health, lifestyle, genetic, and imaging data from over 500,000 volunteer participants across the United Kingdom

  • Resource that allows approved researchers to study how genetics, environment, and lifestyle contribute to disease, driving discoveries that improve prevention, diagnosis, and treatment of a wide range of health conditions

Genomic data source

FinGen - large research project in Finland that combines genetic data from biobank samples with comprehensive national health registry data

  • Resource allows researchers to identify genetic factors that influence health and disease, leveraging Finland’s unique population structure and health records to accelerate medical discoveries and advance precision medicine

Electronic Health Records (EHR) and Clinical Data

Definition and Scope:

  • Electronic Health Records (EHRs) : digital versions of a patient’s chart

  • EHRs are used to collect patient health information in real time

  • Captures a wide array of data, from diagnoses and medications to lab test results, imaging studies, and physician notes

Types of Clinical Data in EHRs:

  • Structured Data

    • Coded fields like age, blood pressure readings, lab results, ICD-10 codes for diagnoses, and medication prescriptions
      • Fields ideal for quantitative analysis

  • Unstructured Data

    • Free-text clinical notes, radiology reports, and discharge summaries
    • Data fields contain rich context > requires natural language processing (NLP) techniques to extract useful information

Electronic Health Records (EHR) and Clinical Data

Uses in Research:

  • Phenotyping patients for studies (e.g., identifying cohorts with diabetes)

  • Observational studies and real-world evidence research

  • Predictive modeling, such as using machine learning to forecast disease progression

Challenges:

  • Data heterogeneity: Different hospitals and EHR systems may encode information differently

  • Missing or incomplete data

  • Privacy and regulatory considerations (HIPAA in the U.S.)

Clinical and Health Data Sources

National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES)

  • Overview

    • comprehensive health and nutrition data from a representative sample of the U.S. population

    • NHANES data helps estimate the prevalence of chronic conditions like diabetes, hypertension, and obesity in the U.S.

  • Key Features:

    • Demographics and Lifestyle: Age, gender, ethnicity, diet, physical activity, and socioeconomic factors

    • Clinical Measurements: Blood pressure, body mass index, bone density, vision and hearing tests

    • Laboratory Tests: Blood and urine analyses for cholesterol, glucose, nutrient levels, environmental chemicals, and more

Clinical and Health Data Sources

County Health Rankings and Roadmaps

  • Overview

    • CHR&R is a program developed by the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation in partnership with the University of Wisconsin Population Health Institute
    • Provides a comprehensive snapshot of health and well-being at the county level across the United States
    • Standardized, publicly available dataset for epidemiologists, health services researchers, and data scientists to study social determinants of health

  • Key Features:

  • Health Outcomes: Measures of how healthy a county is today, including:

    • Mortality: Premature death rates

    • Health Behaviors: Smoking, diet, physical activity, alcohol use

    • Clinical Care: Access to care, quality of care

  • Social & Economic Factors: Education, employment, income, family and social support

Clinical and Pharmaceutical Data

FDA Purple Book

  • Overview

    • Publicly available database maintained by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) that provides information on licensed biological products

    • For Researchers: Provides authoritative information on which biologics are approved and their relationships to biosimilars

  • For Clinicians and Pharmacists: Supports informed decisions about prescribing or substituting biologic therapies

  • Key Features:

    • Biologics and Biosimilars:

    • Lists all FDA-licensed biological products

Clinical and Pharmaceutical Data

FDA Purple Book

  • Approved Drug Products:

    • Lists brand-name and generic drugs, including details such as active ingredients, dosage forms, strengths, and routes of administration

    • Serves as an official record of drugs that have met FDA standards for safety, efficacy, and quality

  • Therapeutic Equivalence Ratings:

    • Evaluates whether a generic drug is therapeutically equivalent to its reference listed drug (RLD)

Sources of Categorical Data

  • Categorical variables are generated by various systems, including:

    • Genomic Science: Genetic variations and classifications

    • Diagnostics: Test results and disease classifications

    • Treatments/Exposures: Medication types and exposure statuses

    • Billing: Codes for procedures and services

Note

These sources produce data that require careful interpretation and standardization

Recap: Health Data Sources

Internal Logic of Categorical Systems

  • Each system has its own rules for generating categorical labels, such as:

    • Valid values and their definitions

    • Ordering of categories

    • Coexistence of multiple values

    • Handling of unknown or missing data

Note

Understanding these rules is crucial for accurate data analysis and integration

Recap: Health Data Sources

Clinical data includes:

  • Structured Data: Numeric or coded information such as vital signs, lab results, ICD-10/ICD-9 diagnoses, and medication prescriptions

  • Unstructured Data: Free-text clinical notes, radiology reports, and discharge summaries. These contain rich context but require processing (e.g., NLP) to extract meaningful insights

Uses:

  • Understanding patient health status and disease progression

  • Phenotyping patient populations for research studies

  • Supporting predictive modeling for outcomes, readmission risks, and treatment response

Note

These clinical data help track health, disease progression, and treatment outcomes, but access is limited by privacy rules and institutional restrictions
